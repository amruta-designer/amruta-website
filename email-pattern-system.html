Email Pattern
Design System

One vision for email. Six conflicting styles replaced, production time cut, and 90% adoption reached across 6 squads.

40% Reduction in production time
80% Render consistency

Role Senior Product Designer, Design Systems
Company Bupa

Problem Space

The Challenge

As the platform grew, fragmented email experiences were slowing down delivery and quietly eroding brand trust.

  • Each squad interpreted guidelines differently, which led to 6 distinct email styles across products with no consistency.

  • No single source of truth. Teams were pulling patterns from legacy files, duplicating effort and drifting further apart.

  • Responsive patterns that held up across desktop, mobile, and dark mode were hard to build without a shared foundation.

  • Every new campaign meant starting from scratch. Slow delivery cycles and unnecessary rework had become the norm.

  • Non-standardised colours and interactions were causing WCAG failures across email templates.

  • Developers were constantly reworking spacing and responsive rules, leading to costly back-and-forth between design and engineering.

  • Brand updates meant manually touching hundreds of templates. There was no token-based update path.

Approach

A structured approach

Four phases took the project from chaotic sprawl to a scalable, well-adopted system.

Phase 01

Research & Audit

Audited 250+ screens to catalogue UI patterns and highlight inconsistencies in colour, spacing, typography, and interactions. Collaborated with engineers to assess dev-side component variations, and ran workshops with squads to surface key pain points.

  • 41% duplicated components across teams
  • Different button styles with no shared spec
  • Spacing tokens ranged from 4px to 16px with no consistency
  • WCAG violations found in 32% of screens
Phase 02

Components & Patterns

Built 12 new patterns on top of existing foundations, tokens, and component library. Added email-specific typography tokens that switch to email-safe fonts by viewport, and local tokens so patterns adapt correctly across different background contexts.

Phase 03

Implementation & Rollout

Rolled out the system to product teams incrementally, with feedback loops built in throughout.

  • Created a Figma Library and Zeroheight documentation
  • Visual examples showing desktop and mobile rendering
  • Conducted onboarding workshops for designers and engineers
  • Established weekly office hours for ongoing support
Phase 04

Documentation & Enablement

To drive adoption, I wrote documentation and ran training so teams could self-serve. Designers and marketers could build on-brand emails without needing specialist support.

Reflections

Key Insights & Learnings

What made the difference between a component library that gets used and one that collects dust.

Start with Research

Understanding existing patterns and pain points was crucial to building a system that truly served team needs. Skipping the audit phase would have meant solving the wrong problems with polished components nobody wanted.

Involve All Stakeholders

Regular collaboration with designers, developers, and product teams ensured genuine buy-in and practical solutions. The weekly office hours were instrumental in catching edge cases early and building community around the system.

Design for Scalability

Building with tokens and modular components means the system can grow without a rebuild. Brand updates now take minutes, not weeks.

Documentation Drives Adoption

The best component library fails if nobody uses it. Clear docs, copy-paste snippets, and regular office hours turned sceptical marketers into advocates. Adoption tracked directly to documentation quality.

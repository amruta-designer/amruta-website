Background

A Platform That Had Outgrown Itself

The LMS had been patched and extended over many years until it became genuinely hard to use. Staff and external users were struggling to find basic information, and the support team was fielding calls that good UX should have made unnecessary.

  • Navigation that defeated users

    Users could not find basic information without calling support. Frequent support requests were a direct symptom of a navigation structure that had never been designed with users in mind.

  • Hidden account information

    Credit totals, renewal dates, and account details were buried or ambiguous. Users had no reliable way to check where they stood without digging through multiple screens.

  • Fragmented progress tracking

    Learning progress and completion data were scattered across different sections with no single view. Users had to piece together their own status from disconnected parts of the platform.

  • Years of accumulated complexity

    Unchecked feature additions over time had created deep layers of duplication and inconsistency. There was no clear map of what the system actually contained or how its parts connected.

Contribution

My Role

Working with the Product Owner and sales team, I led the full design effort from research through to final delivery. The scope went beyond screens. It included making sense of a system nobody had fully mapped before.

  • Led user research through desk analysis, market comparison, and feedback collection
  • Translated pain points into concrete design solutions
  • Documented a complete feature inventory with connections to expose system complexity
  • Restructured the information architecture for simpler, more intuitive browsing
  • Produced wireframes and redesigned entry pages
  • Created design standards for the LMS and CMS foundations
  • Designed the visual identity, brand guidelines, and supporting marketing pages

Research

What the Research Revealed

Reviewing user feedback and looking at the competitive landscape made the problems concrete. Three findings kept surfacing across every source we looked at.

  • Users were calling support instead of finding answers themselves

    The navigation was so confusing that users gave up and picked up the phone. Support calls were the most visible signal that the platform's structure was failing people.

  • Account information was practically invisible

    Credit balances and renewal dates were either hidden or presented ambiguously. Users had no confidence in what they were seeing, which eroded trust in the platform overall.

  • Learning progress was impossible to track at a glance

    Completion metrics and progression data were spread across multiple sections. Users had to navigate several screens just to understand where they were in their learning journey.

Process

How We Approached It

Three phases, each building on the last. We started by reframing problems as opportunities, then mapped what actually existed before deciding what to fix.

Phase 01

How Might We Exploration

Instead of jumping straight to solutions, we reframed every problem as a "How Might We" question. This shift kept the team focused on user needs rather than jumping to obvious fixes, and opened up better ideas in the early sessions.

  • HMW questions drafted for each identified pain point
  • Brainstorming sessions anchored around specific user challenges
  • Solutions evaluated against whether they actually addressed the root cause
Phase 02

Mapping the Existing System

Years of additions meant nobody had a clear picture of what the platform actually contained. I built a comprehensive spreadsheet cataloguing every feature and connection, which revealed just how much duplication and fragmentation had built up.

  • Full feature inventory with relationship mapping
  • Duplicate elements identified and flagged for consolidation
  • Related features grouped to reveal natural clusters
  • Findings reviewed with decision-makers to agree on scope
Phase 03

Prioritising What to Fix

With the full picture visible, the team could make informed decisions about where to focus. Not everything needed a redesign. Prioritising the highest-impact problems kept the project manageable and delivered results faster.

  • Critical flows identified through collaborative review
  • Priorities set based on user impact and delivery feasibility
  • IA restructuring scoped to the most-used pathways first
Phase 04

Design and Delivery

Wireframes, page redesigns, and a new design system, all built around the restructured IA and validated against the original research findings. The visual identity and brand guidelines were developed alongside the product work.

  • Wireframes covering key user journeys
  • Entry pages and dashboard redesigned first
  • Reusable components and design tokens established
  • Brand guidelines and marketing pages delivered alongside the product

Outcomes

What We Delivered

The redesign addressed each of the core problems identified in research. The result was a platform people could actually use, with a design foundation that teams could build on going forward.

Clearer Information Architecture

Consolidating duplicated functionality made it far easier to find what you needed. Support requests tied to navigation dropped as users started finding answers themselves.

Better User Experience

Consistent patterns, improved navigation flows, and clearer account and progress displays gave users the clarity the old platform had been missing.

Design System Foundations

Reusable components, type treatments, colour decisions, and interaction standards were documented so future work could build on a shared base rather than starting from scratch each time.

Brand Identity and Marketing Support

Alongside the product work, complementary brand documentation and promotional materials gave the wider business a consistent visual language to work from.

Reflections

What I Learned

Legacy systems carry lessons that greenfield projects never teach you. Six months in a platform that nobody had fully mapped before left me with a different set of instincts.

Legacy products hide structural complexity

Years of additions create nested feature layers that are invisible until you map them out. Building a complete inventory before designing anything proved essential for actually understanding what we were working with.

HMW framing opens up better solutions

Reframing problems as "How Might We" questions during the redesign turned blockers into design opportunities. It kept the team thinking about possibilities rather than defaulting to the most obvious fix.

User feedback surfaces what metrics miss

Direct feedback mechanisms uncovered issues that usage data alone never flagged. Numbers tell you something is broken; talking to users tells you why.

Cluttered UX often reflects unclear internal priorities

The disorganised platform was a symptom of unguided feature expansion over time. It reinforced how important regular structural review is, not as a one-off project but as ongoing practice.
